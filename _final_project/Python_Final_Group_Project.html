Introduction:¶

The 2020 elections included the presidential, Senate, and House elections. Analyzing voter data and trends is vital for understanding the political climate and the changing times. The goal of this data analysis is to compare voter party data with the party that won that state. We are going to be looking at whether independent voter proportions are influenced by the margin by which the state votes red or blue. To further analyze the data, we want to compare the presidential party that won in the state with the party that won in the Senate and House and see if trends arise. Along with these, we also want to see if there are trends specifically related to presidential swing states, such as how the Senate and House votes compare to which party the states end up choosing in the presidential election. Finally, we wanted to compare whether the margin of winning the presidential election has an effect on the Senate and House party voting proportions. These analyses are relevant since it allows us to see the influences of independent votes and swing states.

After this analysis, we found that there is a higher proportion of independent voters when republicans win the presidential election, but there is a general trend where, as the margin of winning increases, the proportion of independent voters also increases. When matching the state’s party in the presidential election with the other elections, we found that political parties match the state’s presidential, house, and governor votes, but not the senate votes. An interesting finding we found for swing state data was that half of the swing states agreed when comparing presidential and House party or presidential and Senate party, but only ⅛ of the swing states agreed for all three parties. There were no obvious patterns when looking at the House and Senate party voting proportions compared to what the state ended up voting for in the presidential election, but there was a slight trend in the state voting for the same party through all three institutions. Finally, after looking at the margin of victory in the presidential election compared to the proportion of party votes in the House, we saw that the party that won would have a higher proportion of the House votes, too. This was expected; however, the results for the Senate surprised us since the opposite party of the one that won the presidential election won the Senate votes. Data Description:¶

One of the datasets we will be using is president_county_candidate. It has 32177 rows and 6 columns. It contains every state and their counties. It shows each county's number of votes for each presidential candidate and who won for each county. The party of each candidate is also listed. Each row is relevant and shows: a state, a county, a presidential candidate, the party of the presidential candidate, the number of votes for that presidential candidate, and if that candidate won in the county.

The "governors_county_candidate" dataset shows state governor election results at the county-level. Each row, or observation, represents a candidate's results in a particular county. The variables include the state and county in which the candidate ran, candidate name, their affiliated political party, total votes the candidate received in that county, and whether they won in that specific county.

Each observation in the "house_candidate" describes voting results for a candidates in a particular district. Unlike the previous datasets, a state can elect more than one house representative. Winning in one district meant representing the state in the house. Additionally, the data is collected at the district-level, so we must clean it by adding a "State" column to group the districts into appropriate states.

The "senate_county_candidate" dataset shows senate election results at the county-level. Each row, or observation, represents a candidate's results in a particular county. The variables include the state and county in which the candidate ran, candidate name, their affiliated political party, total votes the candidate received in that county.

Description of data cleaning:¶

The presidential data is cleaned so it shows the proportion of Democratic, Republican, and Independent votes for each state and the party of the president the state voted for. All parties that are not Democrat or Republican are recoded to Independent. Finally, the winner in each state is decided by comparing the proportion of votes for each party.

Analagous procedures are conducted for the senate and governor datasets as well.

The "house_candidate" dataset only describes the district making it difficult to extract information at the state-level for each winning candidate. We have to sort the districts into appropriate U.S. states and appended the resulting list as a new column labeled "States". Also, we must consider the extraneous "0th district" that doesn't actually belong to any state. Because there are a different number of representatives for each state, we recode for HOUSE MAJORITY, or the political party that a majority of the winners align with.

#The data table produced by this code shows the proportion of Democrat, Republican, and Independent votes for each state and the party of the president the state voted for.
# Code for cleaning the presidential data so it shows Democratic, Republican, and Independent party proportions
states = []
prop_dem = []
prop_rep = []
prop_ind = []
winner = []

for group in pres.groupby("state"): #groupby groups the data by state. group includes the state name and then all its associated data
    state_name = group[0] #grabs the state name of a group
    state_data = group[1] #grabs the data that corresponds to the state name
    states.append(state_name)

    total = 0 #total number of votes
    dem = 0 #number of democrats
    rep = 0 #number of republicans
    ind = 0 #number of independents
    prop_dems = 0 #proportion of democratic votes
    prop_reps = 0 #proportion of republican votes
    prop_inds = 0 #proportion of independent votes
    win = "no one" #party of the president the state voted for

    for votes, party in zip(state_data["total_votes"], state_data["party"]): #loops through "total_votes" and "party" for each state
        total = total + votes #calculates total votes for each state
        if party == "DEM": #these statements count the number of democrat, republican and independent votes respectively
            dem = dem + votes
        elif party == "REP":
            rep = rep + votes
        else:
            ind = ind + votes
    
    prop_dems = dem/total #proportion of democratic votes
    prop_reps = rep/total #proportion of republican votes
    prop_inds = ind/total #proportion of independent votes

    prop_dem.append(prop_dems)
    prop_rep.append(prop_reps)
    prop_ind.append(prop_inds)   

    if prop_dems > prop_reps and prop_dems > prop_inds: #these statements determine which party won
        win = "DEM"
    elif prop_reps > prop_dems and prop_reps > prop_inds:
        win = "REP"
    else:
        win = "IND"

    winner.append(win)

#creates a new dataframe with the proportions of votes for each party and the winner for each state
pres_proportions = pd.DataFrame({"State": states, "Proportion_Democrat": prop_dem, "Proportion_Republican":prop_rep, "Proportion_Independent": prop_ind, "Winner": winner}).round(3)
display(pres_proportions.head())

Description of main columns:¶

  • The presidential dataset contains all 50 states + Washington DC; house has all 50 states but not DC.
  • The senate dataset contains only 34 states, and governor only contains 11.
  • The mean proportion of votes for the Democratic candidate across all states is equal to the mean proportion of votes for the Republican candidate across all states (not greatly Democrat or Republican "leaning")
  • Senate is more democrat "leaning".
  • House is more republican "leaning".
  • Governor is more republican "leaning".

Discussion:¶

In summary, we found that presidential voting does generally reflect overall state political party matching. However, this pattern is not consistent across all offices, as indicated by the weak political party matching among elected senators compared to house representative and governor elections. When investigating other factors, we also find that independent voters and margin of victory between political parties provide additional context. States with a higher margin of victory, essentially a dominant presidential party, and primarily voted Republican, tend to have a higher proportion of independent participation. On the other hand, “swing states,” or those where the race between political parties was highly competitive, reported lower matching rates across offices. In these cases, presidential voting was not helpful in predicting political party alignment for the other government positions.

With this, we can conclude that while presidential voting can be an accurate predictor of the overall state political position in most cases, it becomes less reliable in more nuanced contexts. In this case, we observe that swing states and senatorial elections deviate from presidential patterns. It is important to mention that we are working with limited datasets, so future analysis should further explore possible demographic, economic, or candidate-specific qualities that may have influenced the inconsistencies in party alignments.
